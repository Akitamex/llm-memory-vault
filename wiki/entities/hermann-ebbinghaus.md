Hermann Ebbinghaus (1850-1909) was a German experimental psychologist, and the first researcher to apply rigorous quantitative methods to the study of memory. He held positions at the universities of Berlin and Breslau.

His landmark work, _Über das Gedächtnis_ (1885), reported a series of self-experiments in which he served as both experimenter and sole subject. To avoid the confounding effects of prior knowledge, he invented nonsense syllables, three-letter CVC trigrams such as "DAX" or "BUP", and memorized long lists of them under controlled conditions.

His central metric was the _savings method_: relearn a list to criterion, measure how many trials the relearning required, and express the reduction versus the original as a percentage. This revealed that memory traces persist well below the threshold of free recall, sometimes for weeks.

The resulting _forgetting curve_ showed retention dropping sharply in the first hours after learning, then levelling into a slower exponential decay. This curve underpins the scheduling logic of all modern [[Spaced Repetition]] systems: intervals are set to catch the memory at near-forgetting, where the consolidation benefit of retrieval is highest.

Ebbinghaus also documented the spacing effect, the finding that distributing study across time outperforms massed repetition. See [[A Primer on Spaced Repetition]] for the full account of his methods and the subsequent research they inspired.
