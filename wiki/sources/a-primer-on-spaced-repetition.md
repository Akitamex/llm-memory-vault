This article is an original work written for the llm-memory-vault template, MIT licensed. It synthesizes public-domain research on memory and learning to demonstrate the vault's ingest workflow.

The article covers five areas in sequence: the forgetting curve and its exponential shape; [[Hermann Ebbinghaus]]'s 1885 self-experiments using nonsense syllables and the savings method; the spacing effect and its leading mechanistic accounts; expanding-interval scheduling with rules of thumb for first-review timing and ease-factor adjustment; and the testing effect as a distinct but complementary phenomenon.

**Key claims and their locations:**

- The forgetting curve follows approximately R = e^(-t/S), where S is memory stability. (Section: "The forgetting curve")
- Ebbinghaus used _savings_, not free recall, as his primary measure: the reduction in relearning trials expressed as a percentage. (Section: "The 1885 self-experiments and the savings method")
- Memory traces persist below the threshold of free recall, sometimes for weeks; savings can be measured even when the learner reports no memory. (Section: "The 1885 self-experiments and the savings method")
- Expanding intervals, anchored by an ease factor per item, form the core of the SM-2 algorithm that underlies Anki. (Section: "Expanding-interval scheduling")
- The testing effect and the spacing effect are separable: spacing benefits accrue from distributed study even without retrieval; retrieval benefits accrue from testing even without spacing; the combination is strongest. (Section: "The testing effect")

**Notable quote:**

> "Cover the answer, force recall, then check." (Section: "The testing effect")

This captures the operational implication of the testing effect: a flashcard tool that reveals the answer without demanding retrieval delivers spacing without testing, and the combined benefit requires both.
